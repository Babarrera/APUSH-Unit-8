APUSH Period 8 — Truly Final Complete Study Guide

THE 4 BIG THEMES

1. The U.S. emerged from WWII as a global superpower defending its economic and political ideals

2. A Cold War engulfed the world as the U.S. and USSR competed through ideology, proxy wars, and arms racing

3. The Civil Rights Movement achieved basic equal rights for all Americans regardless of race

4. U.S. mass culture experienced a major shift — post-war affluence met a new anti-establishment counterculture

TRUMAN'S DOMESTIC PRESIDENCY

Republicans won Congress in the 1946 midterms and did not like Truman. They passed the 22nd Amendment limiting presidents to two terms, symbolically repudiating FDR. They rejected almost all of Truman's 21-point program. The only bill that passed was the Employment Act of 1946, and even that was substantially watered down.

The Taft-Hartley Act (1947) was passed over Truman's veto. It outlawed the closed shop, banned secondary boycotts, prohibited union dues from being used for political activities, and required union officials to swear they weren't Communists. Factory workers hated it because it gutted the power unions had gained under the Wagner Act of 1935.

Truman won a shocking upset over Dewey in 1948 — 303 to 189 electoral votes — and then announced his Fair Deal domestic agenda in 1949. The Fair Deal aimed to extend Social Security, raise the minimum wage, create national health insurance, fund education, and advance civil rights. It was almost entirely blocked by Southern Democrats and Republicans. The only real success was raising the minimum wage from 40 cents to 75 cents per hour.

Truman issued a Committee on Civil Rights in 1946 to assess conditions and recommend reforms for Black Americans. He then desegregated the U.S. military via Executive Order 9981 in 1948. A. Philip Randolph was key in pressuring him to do it. The first desegregated war was Korea.

THE COLD WAR — ORIGINS

How did America position for global influence after WWII? Through the Marshall Plan rebuilding Europe, forming NATO, and adopting containment to stop the spread of communism. Unlike after WWI when the U.S. retreated into isolationism, after WWII the U.S. took an active international role — joining the UN in 1945 as a permanent Security Council member. The Bretton Woods Conference (1944) created the IMF and the World Bank to stabilize the global economy. The Soviets rejected these institutions as capitalist tools.

Why did the Cold War start? The U.S. needed a strong free-market Europe and Asia so American goods had markets to sell to. The Soviets wanted a buffer zone against Germany — so they kept troops in Eastern Europe and turned those nations into communist puppet states instead of holding free elections as promised at Yalta. The U.S. and UK saw this as spreading communism. There were also pre-existing tensions — the U.S. didn't recognize the USSR until 1933, Stalin signed the Hitler-Stalin Pact in 1939, and there were disagreements over Eastern Europe and atomic bomb secrecy during WWII.

Why is it "cold"? No direct armed conflict ever happened between the U.S. and USSR themselves. But it involved real wars fought through proxy states — the U.S. and USSR backed opposing sides without fighting each other directly, as in Korea and Vietnam. Best understood as an era from roughly 1945 to 1990.

The 3 steps that escalated it: First, the USSR established satellite states across Eastern Europe. Second, George Kennan's Long Telegram (February 1946) led to the Truman Doctrine — containment, meaning communism stays where it is and won't be allowed to spread. Third, China fell to Mao Zedong in 1949, making the communist threat feel global.

Churchill gave his Iron Curtain speech in Fulton, Missouri in March 1946, describing the divide between free Western Europe and the communist dictatorships of Eastern Europe.

COMMUNISM — WHAT IT IS

Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels wrote The Communist Manifesto. Key principles: a classless society, equal wages regardless of job performed, no private property, the state controls all means of production, and the eventual elimination of currency entirely. The Bolsheviks started the Russian Revolution in 1917. At one point during the Cold War era a full one quarter of the world's population lived under some form of communist rule.

KEY COLD WAR POLICIES

The Truman Doctrine (March 1947) sent $400 million in military and economic aid to Greece and Turkey — the first application of containment. Truman feared that without help both nations would become communist. The reason behind containment was the Domino Theory — if one nation fell to communism, neighboring nations would fall too like dominoes.

The Marshall Plan sent more than $12 billion to rebuild Western Europe and prevent communist expansion there. The Soviets rejected the aid as a capitalist tool. It was a massive success in stabilizing Western democracies.

The Berlin Airlift (June 1948 to May 12, 1949): when the Western allies introduced a new currency called the Deutsche Mark, the Soviets blockaded all traffic into West Berlin. Since West Berlin was located 100 miles inside the Soviet sector it was completely isolated. The U.S. and Britain flew supply planes into the city for nearly a year. If the Soviets had opened fire on the planes it would have been an act of war. The blockade ended May 12, 1949. The cost of the airlift was $224 million.

NATO formed in 1949 — the U.S., UK, France, Canada, and eight other nations agreed that an attack on one was an attack on all. The Soviets responded with the Warsaw Pact in 1955, a mutual defense treaty of eight communist nations. The feud between them persisted for 36 years. The Warsaw Pact officially disbanded in 1991 with the collapse of the Soviet Union.

NSC-68 (1950) called for massive military buildup — up to 20% of GNP on defense. The Army grew to 3.6 million, the defense budget quadrupled to $52 billion. It also expanded covert operations, propaganda through Voice of America, and cultural exchange programs aimed at undermining communist states. The 4 goals of containment from NSC-68: block further Soviet expansion, expose the falsities of Soviet pretensions, induce a retraction of Kremlin influence, and foster the seeds of destruction within the Soviet system.

MAD — Mutually Assured Destruction: both sides stockpiled nuclear weapons knowing that using them would destroy both nations. That paradox kept the Cold War cold. The Cold War gave Americans a growing awareness that the greatest existential threat to humanity is ourselves.

COVERT CIA OPERATIONS UNDER EISENHOWER

Iran (1953, Operation Ajax): the CIA overthrew Prime Minister Mossadegh, who had nationalized British oil. The motivation was protecting Western oil interests. The U.S. installed the Shah and secured 40% oil concessions for American companies. It worked short-term but created deep lasting anti-American resentment.

Guatemala (1954): the CIA overthrew President Arbenz, who pursued land reform threatening United Fruit Company holdings. Motivation was corporate interests plus fear of communism. U.S.-backed forces invaded, Arbenz fell, but it triggered decades of civil war.

Indochina/Vietnam: the U.S. funded France's fight against Ho Chi Minh's communist Vietminh. The Domino Theory drove this — fear that one communist victory would trigger a chain. France surrendered at Dien Bien Phu in 1954, laying the groundwork for the Vietnam War.

EISENHOWER'S PRESIDENCY

Eisenhower won in 1952 partly because of his popularity as a WWII hero and partly because the Republican campaign focused on "K1C2" — Korea, Communism, and Corruption. Truman's approval had fallen to 23%. Eisenhower sealed his victory by declaring "I shall go to Korea" ten days before the election.

Eisenhower's governing style was called the "hidden hand presidency" — appearing passive while quietly directing policy behind the scenes. He was largely laissez-faire: appointed nine businessmen to his cabinet, placed corporate-friendly appointees on the FTC, FCC, and Federal Power Commission, and hesitated to use government spending to fight recessions. But he was not entirely hands-off — he accepted and modestly expanded Social Security, raised the minimum wage, created the Department of Health Education and Welfare, continued farm price supports, and supported the National Defense Education Act after Sputnik.

Critically, a key analytical point your packet makes is that despite publicly preaching free markets and laissez-faire principles, Eisenhower's America was quietly driven by massive government subsidies — the FHA, the GI Bill, and the Interstate Highway Act. Subsidizing prosperity is not laissez-faire, even when it doesn't look like traditional government intervention. This contradiction defines the 1950s economy.

His "New Look" defense policy emphasized nuclear weapons and massive retaliation as a cheaper alternative to large conventional forces. Kennedy later replaced this with flexible response.

In his farewell address (1961) Eisenhower warned about the military-industrial complex — the dangerous alliance between the military establishment and the defense industry. Annual military spending exceeded the net income of all U.S. corporations combined. He feared this complex would push the country toward endless military buildup, distorting national priorities and threatening democracy itself.

THE RED SCARE & McCARTHYISM

In February 1950 Senator Joseph McCarthy claimed he had a list of 205 Communists in the government. He never produced real evidence — ever. He led HUAC investigations targeting Hollywood, labor unions, and military officials. McCarthyism came to mean accusing someone with little or no evidence. He lost credibility when his reckless smear tactics were exposed during the Army-McCarthy hearings.

The Hollywood Ten were unfriendly witnesses who refused to cooperate with HUAC and were blacklisted.

Senator Margaret Chase Smith pushed back in her Declaration of Conscience (June 1, 1950), arguing Americans have the right to hold unpopular beliefs without being labeled Communists, and that the Senate had become a platform for irresponsible sensationalism. She believed the Red Scare was being abused for political gain.

Red Scare infrastructure: the Smith Act made it illegal to advocate violent overthrow of the government. The Internal Security Act of 1950 required Communist organizations to register with the Attorney General's office. The Federal Employee Loyalty Program reviewed workers for "Americanism" — anyone deemed un-American was fired. Industries created blacklists to keep disloyal people from being hired. Loyalty Review Boards spread to states, communities, and private businesses.

Alger Hiss (State Dept. official) was convicted of perjury for alleged spying — Nixon made his name in that HUAC investigation. Julius and Ethel Rosenberg were executed in 1953 for passing atomic secrets to the Soviets.

Anti-communist media was everywhere. TV shows like The Man Called X and I Led Three Lives featured communist spy characters. Films like I Married a Communist and The Red Menace spread the message. Science fiction films used alien invasion plots as metaphors for communist infiltration. Comic books and paperback novels featured Communist villains. The Cincinnati Reds temporarily changed their name to the Redlegs to avoid the communist association with red.

The American Legion staged a mock Communist takeover of Mosinee, Wisconsin on May Day 1950. Communist agents seized the mayor and police chief, set up roadblocks, confiscated property, annulled constitutional rights, forced unpaid labor, and served Soviet food like black bread and potato soup. That evening residents liberated themselves and raised the American flag. It illustrates how deep anti-Communist hysteria ran in everyday American life.

KOREAN WAR (1950–1953)

Korea was divided at the 38th parallel after WWII. The Soviet Union helped North Korea establish a communist government under Kim Il Sung. On June 25, 1950 North Korea invaded South Korea. Approximately 88% of the UN fighting force was supplied by the United States. General Douglas MacArthur commanded the forces. UN and U.S. forces recaptured Seoul (South Korea's capital) and drove the North Koreans back to the 38th parallel. MacArthur then pushed further north, ignoring China's warnings, and China sent massive troops across the Yalu River. MacArthur wanted to bomb and invade China; Truman wanted a limited war. Truman fired MacArthur in April 1951 for insubordination.

Truman never got a declaration of war from Congress — he called it a UN police action. For the next two years both sides went back and forth in a stalemate. The armistice in 1953 left Korea divided at the 38th parallel with a 2.5-mile wide DMZ where no military personnel or equipment is allowed. About 33,629 Americans were killed and 102,000 wounded.

4 effects: very costly in lives and money, strengthened presidential war powers meaning the president can commit forces without Congress, deepened the Cold War mentality, and set the stage for U.S. involvement in Vietnam.

CUBA & THE MISSILE CRISIS

Castro's 1959 revolution created a communist nation 90 miles from Florida. The U.S.-backed Bay of Pigs invasion failed in April 1961. Cuba then turned to the USSR for protection. In August 1962 the Soviets began secretly installing nuclear missiles in Cuba. The U.S. discovered them on October 15.

Kennedy chose a naval quarantine of Cuba — specifically called a quarantine rather than a blockade because a blockade is technically an act of war under international law — rather than an airstrike as his military advisors recommended. On October 22 he announced the threat publicly, causing nationwide panic. Families stockpiled food, water, toilet paper, batteries, and flashlights. Many sought religious confession believing nuclear war was imminent. Protests swept across the nation alongside the panic.

U.S. Ambassador to the UN Adlai Stevenson revealed photographic evidence that forced the Soviet Union to publicly admit the missile bases existed.

The tensest moment: on October 27 a U.S. Navy vessel hit a nuclear-armed Soviet submarine with a depth charge. The commanders, unable to communicate with the surface, thought war had started and prepared to launch a nuclear torpedo. Only Vasili Arkhipov, the second-in-command, refused to authorize the launch — saving the world. The U.S. reached DEFCON 2 (one step from nuclear war) for the first time in history, with hundreds of nuclear missiles ready to launch.

Resolution: RFK secretly met with Soviet Ambassador Dobrynin and negotiated a deal — the U.S. would remove its missiles from Turkey and Italy and promise never to invade Cuba; in exchange the Soviets would remove their Cuban missiles. Khrushchev accepted. The crisis ended October 28, 1962. Who won? It is debatable — the Soviets removed their Cuban missiles, which looks like a U.S. victory, but the U.S. also secretly gave up missiles in Turkey, so both sides compromised. It remains the closest the U.S. has ever come to nuclear war.

VIETNAM WAR

Background: France withdrew from Indochina after Dien Bien Phu (1954). The Domino Theory held that if one country fell to communism its neighbors would follow — shaped by WWII, when people saw how Nazi expansion worked and believed communism had to be stopped the same way early. Ho Chi Minh was both a communist and a nationalist fighting for liberation from French colonial rule — he invoked the same ideals as the American Revolution. The U.S. could only see him through a Cold War lens as a Soviet tool, ignoring his nationalist motivations entirely.

The Gulf of Tonkin Resolution (1964) gave LBJ a blank check for military escalation after a reported North Vietnamese attack on U.S. ships. The second attack never actually happened — the National Security Agency later admitted this. Johnson announced only two Marine battalions going to Danang publicly, but six weeks later 50,000 combat troops were in Vietnam, and by November 1965 the total topped 165,000. This gap between what was announced and what actually happened is a key example of government deception.

The U.S. strategy of attrition relied on heavy artillery and air power. Search-and-destroy missions attacked villages and turned as many as 4 million people — roughly one quarter of South Vietnam's population — into refugees at any given time. By late 1968 the U.S. had dropped more than 3 million tons of bombs and used Agent Orange in the most destructive chemical warfare in history. The weekly American body count rose from 26 per week in 1965 to 180 per week by 1967.

The credibility gap: starting with a CBS News report in August 1965, television showed Americans the reality — Marines burning civilian homes, cluster bombs releasing 180,000 fiberglass shards, the effects of defoliants. Senator Fulbright, who had helped pass Gulf of Tonkin, became the war's most vocal congressional critic. The war cost $21 billion per year, driving inflation and forcing tax surcharges on individuals and corporations.

The Tet Offensive (January 30, 1968): 85,000 North Vietnamese and Vietcong troops launched surprise attacks on 100+ South Vietnamese cities during the Vietnamese New Year. The Battle of Hue lasted over three weeks — 2,800 bodies were found massacred, 150 Marines were killed. The U.S. lost 4,000 troops total in the offensive. North Vietnam lost over 40,000. It was a military defeat for North Vietnam but shattered public confidence — 49% of Americans now called the war a mistake. LBJ's approval hit an all-time low and he announced he would not seek reelection.

The My Lai Massacre (March 16, 1968): Charlie Company was sent on a search-and-destroy mission to My Lai expecting Vietcong fighters. They found only civilians — women, children, and elderly — and massacred an estimated 300 to 500 villagers. Even a wounded three-year-old was killed. Soldier Ron Ridenhour wrote a letter demanding a government investigation after hearing corroborating accounts from multiple witnesses. Lieutenant William Calley ordered and carried out the killings. When the story became public it devastated American trust in the military and deepened the anti-war movement.

Student protests: civil rights activists returning from Freedom Summer sparked the Free Speech Movement at Berkeley in 1964 — nearly 1,000 people were arrested in the largest mass arrest in California history at that time. The SDS (Students for a Democratic Society) released the Port Huron Statement (1962) stressing participatory democracy and challenging the norm that communism must be stopped at all costs. Three weeks after Operation Rolling Thunder began, teach-ins spread across the U.S., Europe, and Japan. The SDS mobilized 20,000 in an antiwar march on Washington. The April 1967 Central Park rally drew more than 300,000. Muhammad Ali refused the draft, was sentenced to five years, and was stripped of his boxing title. Thousands burned draft cards despite laws providing for five-year jail terms and $10,000 fines. Vietnam Veterans Against the War encouraged returning soldiers to throw off their medals.

Kent State (May 4, 1970): students were protesting Nixon's expansion of the war into Cambodia. The National Guard was sent in after vandalism and looting. On May 4 nearly 3,000 students gathered despite a ban on demonstrations. 28 guardsmen fired 61 to 67 shots, killing four students and injuring nine. 100,000 people marched on the White House on May 9. Less than a week later at Jackson State University in Mississippi, city and state police opened fire on students, killing two and injuring twelve.

As many as 40% of the 8 million who served in Vietnam returned with drug dependencies or PTSD. Soldiers put peace symbols on helmets, refused orders, and sometimes fragged (killed) reckless officers as the war dragged on.

The draft was deeply unfair — college and graduate school enrollment earned a deferment, so the burden of fighting fell on poor, working-class, Black, and Latino men. The average soldier was 19 years old. Local draft boards composed of veterans evaluated who served. A 1969 lottery using 366 capsules with birthdates tried to make it fairer. Nixon eventually ended the draft entirely with an all-volunteer force.

Nixon's plan was Vietnamization, formally called the Nixon Doctrine — gradually shift fighting to South Vietnamese forces while withdrawing U.S. troops, seeking peace with honor alongside advisor Henry Kissinger. He also secretly escalated by bombing Cambodia. The War Powers Act (1973) required the president to notify Congress before military action, restoring the balance of power and effectively overturning the Gulf of Tonkin Resolution. The U.S. withdrew, Saigon fell in 1975, and Vietnam was reunified as a communist nation — the first war the U.S. definitively lost.

Total costs: 58,000 Americans killed, $100+ billion spent, 3 to 4 million Vietnamese dead. It destroyed American trust in the government, destabilized Cambodia and helped the Khmer Rouge rise to power, diverted funding from LBJ's Great Society, caused high inflation in the late 1960s and 70s, and led directly to the 26th Amendment lowering the voting age to 18 — the argument being that if 18-year-olds could be drafted to die they should be able to vote.

THE SPACE RACE

Sputnik 1 launched October 4, 1957 — a large metal sphere about the size of a beach ball, the first artificial satellite in orbit. Americans panicked, fearing the Soviet education system had surpassed theirs. Within four months the first U.S. satellite, Explorer 1, achieved orbit. NASA was created to more effectively pursue American goals in space. The National Defense Education Act also followed, providing federal aid to education in response to Sputnik.

Soviet cosmonaut Yuri Gagarin became the first human to orbit the planet in April 1961. One month later Alan Shepard became the first American in space. On May 25, 1961 Kennedy announced the goal of landing a man on the moon before the end of the decade. In January 1967 three American astronauts were killed in a fire aboard Apollo 1. On July 20, 1969 Neil Armstrong became the first human to set foot on the moon.

CIVIL RIGHTS MOVEMENT

The movement actually started during WWII with efforts like A. Philip Randolph's Double-V campaign — but the 1950s is when they started winning major victories.

The promises that were suppressed: after the Civil War, constitutional amendments guaranteed voting rights and equal protection for Black Americans. Jim Crow laws, poll taxes, literacy tests, and Plessy v. Ferguson (1896) — which upheld racial segregation as constitutional under separate but equal — destroyed those promises for generations.

The NAACP's strategy under Thurgood Marshall was to start with professional and graduate schools where the inequality between Black and white institutions was most obvious and hardest to defend, then work down to undergraduate and K-12 education.

Key milestones: Jackie Robinson broke MLB's color barrier on April 15, 1947 (Brooklyn Dodgers), symbolizing early cracks in Jim Crow segregation. Truman issued Executive Order 9981 in 1948 desegregating the military.

Brown v. Board of Education (1954): Thurgood Marshall argued that segregated schools created a badge of inferiority, supported by psychological research showing damage to Black children's self-esteem. Chief Justice Earl Warren wrote the unanimous ruling that racially separate schools are inherently unequal, overturning Plessy v. Ferguson and ordering desegregation with all deliberate speed.

Southern resistance took two forms: Massive Resistance, led by Senator Harry Byrd of Virginia, encouraged Southern schools to shut down rather than desegregate. Prince Edward County, Virginia closed its public schools from 1959 to 1964 rather than integrate. Some states funded private white academies and flew the Confederate Battle Flag over state capitols in direct response to Brown. The Southern Manifesto (March 1956) was signed by 96 members of Congress, protesting Brown and arguing the Supreme Court overstepped its authority since the Constitution never mentions education.

The 24th Amendment was proposed by Congress in 1962 to abolish the poll tax, which had been used to suppress Black voter participation.

Emmett Till (August 1955): a 14-year-old Black boy from Chicago was murdered in Mississippi after being accused of flirting with a white woman. His mother demanded an open casket to expose the brutality. The two white defendants were acquitted by an all-white jury in under two hours. His death galvanized the civil rights movement.

Montgomery Bus Boycott: Rosa Parks was a veteran NAACP activist and secretary — not simply a tired woman on a bus. She had attended civil rights training in 1954. She refused her seat on December 1, 1955 and was arrested. Black churches organized the boycott and the Women's Political Council used telephone trees to spread the word. 26-year-old MLK Jr. emerged as leader, drawing on Jesus, Thoreau, and Gandhi's nonviolence. The boycott lasted 381 days with thousands walking miles daily. Result: Montgomery buses were desegregated in 1956.

Little Rock Nine (1957): Arkansas Governor Orval Faubus used the National Guard to block nine Black students from entering Little Rock High School. Eisenhower sent the 101st Airborne Division to escort them — the first federal military intervention to protect Black rights since Reconstruction. Eisenhower personally disagreed with Brown but enforced it anyway. By the end of the 1950s fewer than 2% of Black students in the South attended integrated schools.

Greensboro sit-ins (1960): young activists entered whites-only lunch counters and demanded service. They faced mass arrests which made front-page news, eventually pressuring restaurants to change policies. This sparked 70,000+ participants across the South and led to the formation of SNCC (Student Nonviolent Coordinating Committee), giving young people a major organized role in the movement.

Freedom Rides tested the desegregation of interstate buses, provoking violent reactions that drew national attention.

Birmingham (1963): Public Safety Commissioner Bull Connor ordered police to use high-pressure fire hoses and police dogs against peaceful protesters, including children in the Children's Crusade. Footage of this won massive public sympathy for the movement.

March on Washington (1963): over 200,000 activists gathered at the Lincoln Memorial where MLK delivered his I Have a Dream speech, calling for a society defined by racial equality. This galvanized public support and pressured Kennedy to push for civil rights legislation.

Civil Rights Act (1964): banned discrimination in public accommodations and employment based on race, religion, or sex.

Voting Rights Act (1965): eliminated literacy tests and poll taxes, authorized federal oversight of counties with historically low Black voter turnout, and dramatically expanded Black voter registration.

Loving v. Virginia (1967): Supreme Court struck down state laws banning interracial marriage.

MLK was assassinated in April 1968. This gave the nation pause but did not stop the movement, and led some toward more radical separatist groups.

MLK vs. Malcolm X: MLK believed in nonviolent civil disobedience and racial integration, working within the American system, influenced by Thoreau and Gandhi. Malcolm X initially advocated Black separatism and self-defense, arguing Black Americans should build independent institutions rather than seek integration with white society.

Black Power and Black Panthers: Stokely Carmichael began advocating Black Power in 1966 as a way for Black Americans to control their own communities. The Black Panther Party was founded in Oakland in 1966 by Huey P. Newton and Bobby Seale. They demanded land, bread, housing, education, clothing, and justice. They monitored police, ran free breakfast programs for children, established medical clinics, and conducted educational classes. They were heavily persecuted by police and the FBI, with leaders arrested and sentenced to long prison terms.

JFK vs. LBJ for civil rights: LBJ was more impactful — he actually signed the landmark legislation. JFK inspired the movement but was assassinated before it passed.

Urban Uprisings (1965–1968): racial conflict and inequality were not just a Southern issue — they were a nationwide crisis. Urban uprisings broke out across dozens of American cities concentrated heavily in Midwest and Northeast cities like Detroit, Newark, Chicago, and Cleveland, where Black communities struck out against symbols of white control such as white-owned businesses. Major uprisings occurred in Los Angeles (Watts) in 1965 and Detroit and Newark in 1967. These showed that the civil rights victories of the South had not addressed the economic inequality and police brutality faced by Black Americans in northern and midwestern cities.

KENNEDY'S PRESIDENCY

Kennedy won in 1960 by only about 100,000 popular votes — the narrowest margin at that time. His telegenic appearance and confident performance in the first-ever televised presidential debates made Nixon look nervous and unshaven. Kennedy also explicitly promised to keep church and state separate to ease anti-Catholic fears.

Kennedy's New Frontier aimed to inspire a new sense of national purpose beyond just enjoying prosperity, calling on Americans — especially young people — to pursue idealism and serve the nation.

The Bay of Pigs (1961) was a failure. The Cuban Missile Crisis (1962) is seen as mixed — the Soviets removed their missiles from Cuba, which looks like a U.S. win, but the U.S. also secretly agreed to remove missiles from Turkey, so both sides gave something up.

Kennedy's assassination produced some of the most powerful images of the era — young John F. Kennedy Jr. saluting his father's casket captured personal grief alongside national mourning. Jack Ruby shooting Lee Harvey Oswald on live television was the first nationally televised murder.

LBJ'S GREAT SOCIETY

LBJ wanted to build on Kennedy's legacy and wage an unconditional war on poverty. What made the Great Society possible was a specific combination of factors — LBJ used the political momentum of the civil rights movement combined with Democratic majorities in Congress to push through the most ambitious reform program since the New Deal. The cornerstone was the Economic Opportunity Act of 1964, which created the Office of Economic Opportunity to coordinate community-based programs helping the poor through education and employment.

Key legislation: the Civil Rights Act (1964), Voting Rights Act (1965), Medicare and Medicaid through the Social Security Act of 1965 which funded healthcare for elderly Americans and medical care for welfare recipients, the Elementary and Secondary Education Act, and the creation of the Department of Housing and Urban Development.

The data showed the stakes clearly — Black Americans consistently had worse life expectancy, higher infant mortality, and far greater poverty rates than white Americans, and the racial gap never closed during this period. The explanation is that slavery and Jim Crow had for generations denied Black Americans access to education, jobs, healthcare, and wealth.

The Great Society was undermined by Vietnam — the war cost $21 billion per year, driving inflation and forcing tax surcharges, draining the domestic agenda. LBJ chose not to run for reelection in 1968.

1968 — YEAR OF TURMOIL

The Tet Offensive destroyed LBJ's credibility (covered above). The Democratic National Convention in Chicago: Mayor Daley refused parade permits to antiwar protesters and sent undercover police to incite violence so retaliation would seem justified. Police staged what a presidential commission later called a police riot, randomly assaulting demonstrators, passersby, and television crews. Tear gas drifted into the convention hall itself. Senator Ribicoff protested the Gestapo tactics of Chicago police on the convention floor while crowds outside chanted "the whole world is watching" — and through satellite transmission, it literally was.

MLK and RFK were both assassinated in 1968. Nixon won the election with 301 electoral votes to Humphrey's 191. Segregationist George Wallace (American Independent Party) won 46 electoral votes and over 9 million popular votes — showing the depth of white backlash against civil rights progress. Democrats lost most northern states that had voted Democratic since FDR. The result reflected a nation deeply divided over Vietnam, race, and social change. Nixon won using the Southern Strategy, deliberately appealing to white southern voters uncomfortable with civil rights progress.

RICHARD NIXON

Nixon pursued Vietnamization/the Nixon Doctrine (covered above). He opened diplomatic relations with China in 1972 — the first U.S. president to do so — exploiting the Sino-Soviet split. He negotiated SALT I to limit nuclear arsenals. He created the EPA in 1970. His approach to the Cold War was détente — negotiation rather than confrontation, with Henry Kissinger as his key advisor.

The Pentagon Papers — a classified history of the war leaked by Daniel Ellsberg — revealed the government had systematically lied to the public about Vietnam. Published by the New York Times and Washington Post in 1971.

Watergate: Nixon's operatives broke into Democratic headquarters at the Watergate complex. Nixon covered up his involvement. When the cover-up was exposed through press reporting and Senate hearings, he faced certain impeachment and became the only U.S. president ever to resign, in August 1974. Gerald Ford pardoned him.

SALT TALKS

The first negotiations between the U.S. and USSR to limit nuclear arms occurred in Helsinki, Finland in 1969. Over the next three years these became known as the Strategic Arms Limitation Talks. The first real progress came in 1971 with an agreement to limit anti-ballistic missile systems. In 1972 both countries agreed to dismantle older ICBM launch bases — this was SALT I. SALT II discussions took place between Carter and Soviet Premier Brezhnev, producing promises to limit production of new nuclear weapons. Neither nation fully honored the agreement. Six months after SALT II was signed, Soviet troops invaded Afghanistan, straining relations. The U.S. Senate never ratified SALT II. Reagan formally withdrew the U.S. from SALT II in 1986 after accusing the Soviets of violating it.

FORD & CARTER

Ford pardoned Nixon. The fall of Saigon (1975) marked communist victory in Vietnam on his watch. Carter brokered the Camp David Accords (1978), a peace deal between Israel and Egypt. He failed to resolve the Iranian hostage crisis (444 days). SALT II collapsed after the Soviet invasion of Afghanistan in 1979. His weak presidency set the stage for Reagan.

POSTWAR ECONOMY & SUBURBANIZATION

The GI Bill (Servicemen's Readjustment Act of 1944) gave veterans college tuition, low-interest home loans, and business startup funds — helping millions enter the middle class. Between 1946 and 1960 the gross national product more than doubled and wages rose for ordinary working people. By 1960 an estimated 60% of Americans enjoyed a middle-class standard of living.

The baby boom (1945–1960) added 50 million people to the U.S. population as returning veterans started families, driving massive demand for housing and consumer goods.

Suburbanization: the number of U.S. homes doubled during the 1950s. Levittown — William Levitt mass-produced homes in New York, housing 40,000 people almost overnight — became the symbol of postwar suburbanization. It was largely a white middle-class phenomenon — minorities were often excluded through redlining and FHA discrimination.

The FHA insured long-term mortgages with low down payments and 30-year loans, fueling the home-building boom but reinforcing racial and income segregation by redlining minority neighborhoods.

The Interstate Highway Act (1956) built a national highway system, accelerating suburban commuting, boosting the auto and construction industries, and facilitating migration across the country.

By 1960, 80% of Americans owned at least one car. Car culture produced shopping malls and drive-through restaurants, reshaping American consumer life.

The Sun Belt (southern and western states) grew as Cold War defense spending shifted there, moving political power away from the Northeast and Midwest.

The key analytical point: despite Eisenhower publicly preaching free markets, the postwar boom was quietly driven by massive government subsidies — the FHA, the GI Bill, and the Interstate Highway Act. Subsidizing prosperity is not laissez-faire, even when it doesn't look like traditional government intervention.

1950s MASS CULTURE

Mass culture was a widespread homogeneous set of ideas and patterns of behavior spread partly through McCarthyism — people feared being labeled a non-conformist or communist suspect if they stepped out of line. The Cold War created pressure to conform.

Television reached 90% of U.S. households by the end of the 1950s. A few networks aired sitcoms like Leave It to Beaver and Father Knows Best, sports, soap operas, and variety shows, creating a common national language and shared values. TV also transformed politics — Senator Estes Kefauver became a national figure through televised crime hearings, Nixon's Checkers speech used emotional TV appeal to save his career, 1952 saw the first TV presidential ads, and the Kennedy-Nixon debates showed that image now mattered above all else in American electoral politics.

Advertising rose alongside TV. With postwar prosperity and more disposable income, advertisers appealed to emotional needs like belonging and status rather than survival needs. Credit cards let people buy more than they could afford, fueling consumer culture. The teen consumer market was enormous — teens spent roughly $10 billion per year by 1959. Businesses rushed to sell them clothing, cosmetics, records, and cars. Advertisers also found that teens influenced large family purchases like cars and appliances.

Rock 'n' roll was rooted in Black musical tradition — Chuck Berry, Little Richard, Fats Domino. White-owned labels made inferior cover versions of Black artists' songs to sell to white audiences, profiting from Black musical innovation while the original artists were pushed aside. Elvis Presley became the breakout white rock star, blending Black R&B with country music at a time of strict racial segregation — his career personified the tension between Black cultural expression and white commercial success that defined postwar American life. DJ Alan Freed popularized the term rock 'n' roll. Older generations opposed it as rebellious and sexual.

Critics of the conformist 1950s: C. Wright Mills (The Power Elite — a small elite of military, corporate, and political leaders made democracy an afterthought), David Riesman (The Lonely Crowd — Americans were conformist other-directed people who cared more about fitting in than thinking for themselves), John Kenneth Galbraith (questioned a society that funded cars and missiles but not schools).

The Beat Generation: Jack Kerouac's On the Road used free-form stream-of-consciousness style rejecting standard meter and championing spontaneity. J.D. Salinger's Catcher in the Rye was a literary challenge to mass culture conformity.

The counterculture: hippies gathered in San Francisco's Haight-Ashbury district in communal living, embracing marijuana, LSD, free love, and rebellious music and clothing as a rejection of their parents' values. Bob Dylan was a key musical voice. Woodstock (1969) drew nearly 400,000 people featuring Jimi Hendrix, Crosby Stills Nash and Young, and Joan Baez. The movement fizzled by the early 1970s due to its own excesses.

SDS and the New Left: Tom Hayden created the New Left in 1961. The Port Huron Statement (1962) stressed participatory democracy. College students opposed Vietnam because after graduation they faced being drafted into what many saw as an immoral war.

SOCIAL MOVEMENTS (1960s–70s)

Women's Rights: 1950s culture confined women to the home — they lacked equal pay and opportunity despite serving in the workforce during both World Wars. Betty Friedan's The Feminine Mystique (1963) exposed the boredom and imprisonment of suburban housewives and sold over a million copies, leading to the founding of NOW in 1966. The Equal Pay Act helped. Title IX (1972) banned gender discrimination in education and secured funding for women's sports. Roe v. Wade (1973) ruled states couldn't ban abortion in the first two trimesters, based on a right to privacy under the 14th Amendment's due process clause. The Equal Rights Amendment fell two votes short of ratification because Phyllis Schlafly's STOP ERA campaign argued it would remove privileges like Social Security dependent wife benefits, separate bathrooms, and draft exemption.

Environmental Movement: Rachel Carson's Silent Spring (1962) exposed the dangers of DDT pesticides and argued modern society was poisoning the earth. The Cuyahoga River in Ohio caught fire in 1969 from heavy industrial pollution. Nixon created the EPA in 1970 to manage pollution control, regulate polluting industries, and oversee pesticide use. The Clean Air Act (1970), first Earth Day (1970), and Clean Water Act (1972) followed. On energy: Arab OPEC nations cut oil exports in 1973 in response to U.S. support for Israel in the Yom Kippur War, causing fuel shortages and price spikes and exposing U.S. dependence on foreign oil. Nuclear energy was proposed as an alternative — uranium was plentiful and produced no greenhouse gases — but the 1979 Three Mile Island partial meltdown in Pennsylvania released radioactive waste into a heavily populated area and fueled public fear about nuclear safety.

Latino Rights: César Chávez and Dolores Huerta founded the United Farm Workers in 1962 to protect underpaid Mexican migrant farm workers. A 1965 strike against grape growers in California's San Joaquin Valley led to a nationwide grape boycott by 1968. By 1970 they had won increased wages. The Brown Berets helped plan a 1968 walkout that sent nearly 15,000 Chicano teenagers into the streets of East Los Angeles demanding educational reform including courses on Mexican American history, literature, art, and language.

AIM (American Indian Movement): founded in 1968 to represent urban Indian communities, reclaim heritage, achieve self-determination, and address systemic poverty. On November 20, 1969 eighty-nine Indians of All Tribes occupied Alcatraz Island claiming it under an 1868 Sioux treaty, staying for 19 months. In 1972 AIM sponsored the Trail of Broken Treaties caravan to Washington DC. In spring 1973 they occupied Wounded Knee, South Dakota, demanding restoration of treaty rights in a siege that lasted ten weeks, leaving two Indian activists dead. This led to the Indian Self-Determination Act of 1975, giving tribes control over lands, education, and law enforcement.

Gay Liberation: in the 1950s and 60s homosexuality was illegal in 49 states. On June 27, 1969 New York police raided the Stonewall Inn in Greenwich Village, provoking an uprising that lasted the entire night. The Gay Liberation Front formed, declaring itself a revolutionary group rejecting society's attempt to impose sexual roles. First Gay Pride parades were held in at least eight American cities in 1970. By 1973 the American Psychiatric Association reclassified homosexuality as a normal sexual orientation. In 1975 the U.S. Civil Service Commission ended its ban on employing homosexuals. Obama designated Stonewall a national monument in 2016.

RISE OF CONSERVATISM

Conservatives had three goals: stop moral and cultural decline, limit federal government and restore states' rights, and push for more aggressive foreign policy. William F. Buckley founded the National Review in 1955 to unify conservative thought. Barry Goldwater's 1964 campaign made states' rights a conservative staple even though he lost badly. Nixon's Southern Strategy in 1968 deliberately appealed to white southern voters. The 1970s saw evangelical Christianity merge with conservative politics. Jerry Falwell founded the Moral Majority in 1979, endorsing candidates who supported family values and convincing millions that voting Republican was voting for God. James Dobson founded Focus on the Family. These movements laid the foundation for Ronald Reagan's election and modern American conservatism.

EXAM TIPS

For essays: compare civil rights protests to anti-war protests, compare MLK and Malcolm X to earlier civil rights figures, and analyze how WWII simultaneously created conditions for both the Cold War and the Civil Rights Movement. Also be ready to discuss how government subsidies quietly drove the postwar boom despite public rhetoric about free markets.

For MCQ: Brown overturned Plessy. Eisenhower enforced Little Rock despite personally disagreeing with Brown. MLK was influenced by Thoreau and Gandhi. The Gulf of Tonkin second attack never happened. Nixon was the only president to resign. The Cold War was cold because the two superpowers never fought each other directly. Containment was George Kennan's idea. The Marshall Plan rebuilt Western Europe. NSC-68 quadrupled the defense budget. The Domino Theory justified Vietnam. Tet was a military defeat for North Vietnam but a political disaster for LBJ. The War Powers Act (1973) restored congressional checks on presidential war powers. The 26th Amendment lowered the voting age to 18. Bretton Woods created the IMF and World Bank. The Nixon Doctrine is another name for Vietnamization. A quarantine is legally distinct from a blockade. Urban uprisings showed racial inequality was a national not just Southern problem.
